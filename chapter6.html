CHAPTER VI

The Name Above Every Name

Every story in Scripture moves toward revelation.

The earliest pages introduce a God who creates by speaking, who walks with Adam in the cool of the day, who calls Abraham from an unfamiliar land and makes covenant with a people who possess nothing except His promise. Through centuries of wandering, exile, deliverance, and restoration, He remains faithful while revealing Himself one step at a time. Prophets describe His holiness with language that strains against human limitation. Kings write songs that celebrate His mercy. Priests stand before altars that testify to His presence, yet every revelation leaves the reader waiting for something still unseen.

The Old Testament is filled with expectation.

Isaiah speaks of a child who will be called Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace. Jeremiah announces a new covenant written upon the heart. Ezekiel sees a shepherd gathering scattered sheep. Zechariah looks toward a King who enters Jerusalem in humility while carrying the hope of the nations.

Each promise adds another brushstroke to the same portrait.

John opens his Gospel by stepping behind every prophecy and every promise.

“In the beginning was the Word.”

The sentence reaches farther than Bethlehem and farther than Abraham. Before there was a chosen nation, before there was a tabernacle or a temple, before the first star occupied its place in the heavens, there was God.

Then John writes words that still possess the power to stop the reader.

“And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us.”

The revelation toward which Scripture has been moving suddenly stands in the middle of ordinary life. The invisible becomes visible. The eternal enters time. The Creator walks the roads of Galilee, speaks with fishermen, touches lepers, welcomes children, and forgives sinners.

Nothing in the Gospels makes sense apart from that reality.

Jesus does not merely speak about God.

He reveals Him.

The disciples spend years discovering this truth one conversation at a time. Storms obey His voice without negotiation. Demons recognize Him before religious leaders do. Bread multiplies in His hands. The blind receive sight, the lame rise to their feet, and the dead return to life, yet every miracle points beyond itself toward the One through whom the miracle has come.

By the time Acts begins, the apostles no longer speak of Jesus as a teacher whose memory deserves preservation.

They proclaim Him as Lord.

That confession quietly shapes every page Luke writes.

A lame man sits at the gate called Beautiful, expecting nothing more than another coin from another passerby. Peter stops, looks at him, and offers something entirely different.

“In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth rise up and walk.”

The miracle changes a man’s life, but Peter refuses to allow the miracle to become a monument to himself. The crowd gathers, astonished by what they have seen, and Peter immediately redirects their attention.

“Why marvel ye at this?”

The question is revealing.

The apostle seems almost determined to disappear from his own story. His hands lifted the man, yet Peter insists that another hand deserves every eye. The healing belongs to Jesus. The power belongs to Jesus. The glory belongs to Jesus.

The same pattern follows the church wherever the gospel travels.

Philip preaches Christ in Samaria.

Paul reasons concerning Christ in the synagogues and marketplaces of the Roman world.

Believers suffer for the name of Christ and rejoice that they have been counted worthy to do so.

The apostles never invite people to admire them. Every road they travel leads toward the same destination.

Perhaps that is why denominational names feel so unfamiliar when placed beside the pages of Acts.

Luke records no movement gathered around Peter, no fellowship identified by Paul, and no community carrying the name of John. The personalities are memorable, but the center never shifts. Every sermon, every miracle, every conversion quietly gathers around the same revelation until the reader begins to notice that the true subject of Acts is not the apostles at all.

It is Jesus Christ.

The church found its identity there.

Its fellowship grew there.

Its worship rose there.

Its hope rested there.

Long before believers were known by traditions, movements, or institutions, they were known by the name they confessed and the Lord they followed. The apostles carried that name into cities that had never heard it, into prisons where chains could not silence it, into homes where strangers became brothers and sisters through its power.

Nothing in Acts suggests that the church ever searched for another foundation.

Having found Jesus Christ, the apostles spent the rest of their lives making Him known, trusting that every heart drawn to Him would discover the same truth that had transformed their own.

The name above every name was never given merely to distinguish the church.

The name was given so that every generation might know the God who came near, walked among us, and still gathers His people to Himself.
