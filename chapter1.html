CHAPTER I

The Question Every Seeker Must Ask

Every Christian belongs to a story that began long before the first church building was raised, the first creed was written, or the first denomination took its name from a movement, a city, or a reformer. Whether a believer worships in a cathedral shaped by centuries of history or in a small congregation meeting in a rented room, every expression of Christian faith eventually traces its roots beyond tradition, beyond institutions, and beyond the memory of generations to a single city and a single day recorded with remarkable simplicity in the book of Acts.

That journey is worth making.

Not because history lacks value, but because history is always strongest when it remains connected to its source. Rivers carry life across valleys and nations, yet every river begins where fresh water first rises from the earth. A branch may stretch in unexpected directions, but every branch depends upon the same unseen root. The farther one travels from the beginning without looking back, the easier it becomes to mistake the course of the river for its source or the branch for the tree itself.

The Christian faith invites a different kind of remembering.

Long before believers identified themselves by denominational names, they were known simply as disciples of Jesus Christ. Long before councils debated doctrine or reformers challenged established traditions, ordinary men and women gathered in Jerusalem with nothing to sustain them except the promise of their risen Lord. They possessed no established institutions, no centuries of accumulated interpretation, and no carefully defined theological systems. They waited because Jesus had told them to wait, trusting that the God who had conquered death would also keep His word.

Luke records those days without embellishment. He does not attempt to persuade the reader that history is approaching a turning point. Merchants still open their shops each morning. Pilgrims still fill the streets for the feast. Priests continue their familiar routines within the temple courts, and families arrive from distant provinces carrying the languages and customs of places scattered throughout the Roman world. Jerusalem appears wonderfully ordinary, moving through another holy season much as it always has.

Yet beneath that ordinary morning another story is quietly unfolding.

Behind closed doors a small company of disciples remains together in prayer, united not by organizational structure or doctrinal debate but by obedience. They are not planning a movement or establishing a religion. They are waiting for a promise. The simplicity of that waiting reveals something easily forgotten by later generations. The church did not begin with strategy. It began with dependence.

That distinction reaches far beyond history.

Every generation inherits forms of worship, familiar language, and cherished traditions that shape the way Scripture is read. Such gifts deserve gratitude, yet gratitude must never become a substitute for examination. A belief may be ancient without being apostolic. A practice may be widespread without appearing in the pages of Acts. The sincere seeker eventually arrives at a question that cannot be answered by custom alone.

Where did the church begin?

The answer cannot be discovered by comparing denominations or measuring the influence of religious movements. It waits where Luke begins his account, before divisions, before institutions, before Christianity becomes associated with names that would have been unknown to Peter, John, James, or Paul.

It waits in Jerusalem.

Everything that follows in this book grows from that single conviction. The purpose is neither to diminish sincere believers nor to elevate one modern movement above another. The purpose is far simpler and, perhaps, far more demanding. We will walk back through history until the familiar voices grow quiet enough for the apostles to speak again. We will stand among the pilgrims gathered in Jerusalem, listen to the first sermon preached after the resurrection of Jesus Christ, and ask whether the church that emerged from that day still provides the pattern by which every generation should measure itself.

Every journey toward truth begins with a question.

The book of Acts invites us to ask the first one again.
