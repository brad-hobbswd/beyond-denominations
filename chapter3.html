CHAPTER III

Peter’s Answer Never Changed

The question asked on the Day of Pentecost deserved only one answer.

Nothing in Luke’s account suggests uncertainty. Peter does not pause to consult the other apostles or invite the multitude into a discussion that might produce several equally faithful responses. The authority of his words rests upon something deeper than personal conviction. He stands as a witness of the risen Christ, speaking with the confidence of a man entrusted with a message that belongs to heaven rather than himself.

That confidence quietly shapes the rest of Acts.

The reader might expect the gospel to evolve as it leaves Jerusalem. New cities bring new cultures. New audiences carry different assumptions. A message first proclaimed to devout Jews during a feast must surely be adjusted before it reaches Samaritans, Roman soldiers, merchants, philosophers, and idol worshippers.

Luke records no such adjustment.

Instead, he invites the reader to watch the gospel travel.

The road first turns north into Samaria, a place marked by centuries of suspicion and division. The distance between Jew and Samaritan was measured by far more than geography. History, worship, and identity had separated the two peoples for generations, yet Philip arrives with no distinctly Samaritan gospel. He preaches Christ, and the city receives the Word with joy.

When Peter and John journey from Jerusalem to Samaria, they bring nothing new with them. They do not explain that the message must now be adapted for a different culture or reshaped for a people with a different history. Their presence confirms rather than corrects what has already been preached, and the narrative continues without the slightest suggestion that another apostolic pattern exists.

The story widens again.

A Roman centurion named Cornelius gathers family and friends within his home, creating a scene that would have seemed unimaginable only a few chapters earlier. Peter himself arrives reluctantly, still learning that the grace of God is larger than the boundaries tradition had drawn around it. Before the sermon ends, the Holy Ghost falls upon those who hear the Word, leaving every Jewish believer present astonished by what God has done.

Yet the miracle does not produce a new message.

Peter does not stand before Gentiles with different instructions from those given in Jerusalem. The God who made no distinction in pouring out His Spirit makes no distinction in the gospel that accompanies the promise. The same apostle who answered a convicted multitude now speaks with the same certainty inside the home of a Roman soldier.

Years later, Paul encounters disciples in Ephesus whose understanding has stopped with the ministry of John the Baptist. He does not congratulate them for their sincerity and leave them where they are. Nor does he suggest that apostolic teaching changes according to the knowledge a person already possesses. He patiently opens the Scriptures, speaks of Jesus Christ, and leads them into the fullness of what God has revealed.

Luke tells these stories with remarkable restraint.

He never stops to argue that the apostles remained united because he never observes anything else. Peter does not preach one gospel while Paul develops another. Philip does not establish an independent movement in Samaria. John never returns with a revised interpretation intended to replace what came before.

The unity is woven into the narrative itself.

Every road leads away from Jerusalem, yet every road carries the same witness. Languages change, rulers change, cities change, and the church grows beyond anything the disciples could have imagined, but the apostolic message moves through history with the quiet consistency of a river remaining faithful to its source.

Perhaps that is the most overlooked testimony in the book of Acts.

The first church spread across continents without surrendering its identity. Diversity appeared in faces, languages, occupations, and customs, but not in the gospel itself. Luke records extraordinary miracles, daring missionary journeys, and astonishing conversions, yet one miracle remains so constant that it almost escapes notice.

The message never changes.

That observation is not merely historical.

It invites every generation to ask whether the church is best served by searching for something new or by returning to what the apostles first proclaimed. The answer waits in the pages of Acts, where the gospel flows steadily from Jerusalem into the world without losing either its clarity or its power, carrying the same invitation to every heart willing to ask the question that first echoed through the streets of the holy city.

“What shall we do?”
