CHAPTER V

One Lord, One Faith, One Baptism

The church was born in a city filled with differences.

Some who stood in Jerusalem had traveled only a few miles to reach the feast. Others had crossed seas and deserts, carrying languages, customs, and memories formed in places far removed from Judea. Luke pauses long enough to name their homelands, allowing the reader to hear the diversity gathered beneath the sound from heaven. Parthians stood beside Medes, visitors from Rome beside pilgrims from Arabia, strangers who might never have met under ordinary circumstances suddenly found themselves listening to the same gospel.

The miracle did not erase their differences.

It gave those differences a new center.

That quiet truth follows the church wherever Acts carries the story. The gospel enters Samaria without becoming Samaritan. It reaches the house of Cornelius without becoming Roman. Paul walks the streets of Ephesus and Corinth without reshaping the apostolic message into something more acceptable to Greek philosophy or the expectations of the empire. New people continually enter the church, yet the church itself remains wonderfully recognizable.

Paul later gives language to what Luke has been demonstrating from the beginning.

“There is one body, and one Spirit… one Lord, one faith, one baptism.”

The words appear almost without explanation, as though the apostle expects every believer to recognize them immediately. He is not presenting an ideal the church might someday achieve. He is describing a reality already woven into its life.

The repetition deserves attention.

One body.

One Spirit.

One hope.

One Lord.

One faith.

One baptism.

The movement is not restrictive. It is unifying. Every phrase draws the reader away from multiplication and back toward the simplicity that marked the church from its beginning. The same Christ who called fishermen beside the Sea of Galilee now gathers Jews and Gentiles, servants and rulers, women and men into a fellowship that could never have existed through human effort alone.

Their unity does not begin with agreement about one another.

It begins with Christ.

That distinction explains much of what follows in Acts. The apostles never spend their ministry creating a common identity through negotiation. They preach Jesus Christ, and those who believe discover that they already belong to one another because they belong to Him.

Perhaps this is why Luke never records competing apostolic movements.

Peter ministers in Jerusalem.

Philip travels through Samaria.

Paul carries the gospel across the Mediterranean world.

Different voices speak in different places, yet no reader reaches the end of Acts wondering whether the apostles proclaimed different faiths. Their personalities vary. Their callings differ. Their audiences change from chapter to chapter. The witness remains remarkably consistent because every apostle stands beneath the authority of the same Lord.

The image is less like separate rivers flowing toward one sea than branches drawing life from one living tree. Each extends in its own direction, reaching people others could never reach, yet every branch remains connected to the same source. Separation from that source would not produce diversity. It would produce death.

History would eventually become more complicated.

Names would appear that honored reformers, cities, confessions, and movements. Communities of sincere believers would preserve traditions treasured for generations, often with deep love for Jesus Christ and genuine devotion to Scripture. Their stories deserve respect, for the history of the church is filled with remarkable faithfulness expressed in countless places.

Acts quietly asks another question.

What existed before those names?

Luke’s answer is almost disarmingly simple. A church gathered around one Lord. A people continuing in one faith proclaimed by the apostles. A baptism received in obedience to the same gospel that first echoed through Jerusalem on the Day of Pentecost.

The simplicity of that picture carries unusual beauty.

Unity is no longer something manufactured through compromise or maintained by human organization. Unity becomes the natural life of a people who have discovered the same Savior and willingly ordered every part of their lives around Him.

Paul’s words therefore sound less like a command than a reminder.

The church does not create one Lord.

The church does not invent one faith.

The church does not establish one baptism.

The church receives them with gratitude, and in receiving them discovers the quiet miracle that has accompanied the gospel from the beginning: strangers become family, not because they have chosen the same tradition, but because they have been gathered by the same Jesus Christ into one body that still bears His name.
