CHAPTER VII

Repentance and New Birth

Some words become so familiar that they are no longer heard.

Repentance is one of them.

For many people the word carries memories of warning rather than invitation, of condemnation rather than mercy. It has been spoken from pulpits, printed on signs, and repeated often enough that its meaning is assumed long before its significance is considered. Yet the New Testament introduces repentance in a remarkably different way. It appears wherever the grace of God meets the human heart.

John the Baptist stood beside the Jordan and called Israel to repent. Jesus began His public ministry with the same invitation, announcing that the kingdom of God was at hand. After the resurrection, Peter stood in Jerusalem before a multitude whose hearts had been awakened by the preaching of Christ, and the first word of his answer remained unchanged.

Repent.

The consistency is difficult to overlook.

Different voices speak in different settings across many years, yet each begins at the same place. The gospel does not first ask what a person knows or where a person has worshipped. It reaches for something deeper, inviting men and women to turn toward God with an honesty that leaves nothing hidden and nothing defended.

Perhaps that is why Luke describes conviction before he records obedience.

The multitude gathered in Jerusalem does not argue with Peter or appeal to inherited tradition. They do not defend the beliefs of their parents or the customs of generations before them. Truth has reached the heart, and once truth arrives, self justification begins to lose its strength.

Repentance is born in that quiet surrender.

Scripture never presents repentance as the improvement of an old life. A house in need of a stronger foundation is not repaired by painting the walls, and a tree bearing bitter fruit is not changed by tying sweeter fruit to its branches. Something deeper must happen, something reaching beneath outward appearance to the place where desire, loyalty, and worship begin.

Jesus chose the language of birth.

His conversation with Nicodemus takes place away from the crowds, under the cover of night, where questions can be asked without interruption. Nicodemus arrives with everything that should have inspired confidence. He knows the Scriptures. He has devoted his life to the service of God. He occupies a respected place among the teachers of Israel.

Jesus speaks as though none of those things can substitute for what is still required.

“Except a man be born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God.”

The image is striking because birth is never an achievement. No child enters the world through effort or education. Life arrives as a gift, creating what did not exist before. Jesus reaches for that image because the kingdom cannot be entered by inheritance, tradition, or religious accomplishment. The beginning must come from God.

Nicodemus hears impossibility.

Jesus reveals promise.

“Except a man be born of water and of the Spirit, he cannot enter into the kingdom of God.”

Luke never pauses in Acts to explain those words because he assumes the reader is watching them unfold. Hearts are convicted. Men and women repent. They are baptized. They receive the promise of the Holy Ghost. The pattern moves with the quiet consistency of seed becoming harvest, each step belonging to the same gracious work of God.

Nothing feels forced.

The apostles do not persuade people into religious improvement.

They bear witness to Jesus Christ, and lives begin again.

Fishermen leave familiar shores. Tax collectors discover mercy greater than their past. A Pharisee becomes an apostle to the Gentiles. Thousands of pilgrims return from Jerusalem carrying more than memories of a feast. The old life gives way to something entirely new, not because human resolve has become stronger, but because the grace of God has reached where human effort never could.

Paul would later write that those who are in Christ are new creatures.

The language is beautifully uncomplicated.

Creation belongs to God.

A new creation belongs to God as well.

The church grew because ordinary people encountered extraordinary grace and found themselves changed from the inside outward. Repentance opened the heart, and the life of Christ entered where self had once ruled.

That invitation remains as gentle and as demanding as it was on the Day of Pentecost.

The gospel does not ask anyone to become a better version of the person they have always been.

It invites every seeker to begin again, trusting that the God who brought forth light from darkness is still able to create new life wherever a heart turns toward Him.
